How tea began to grow...

@ 2023 Front End 2

The Dutch were pioneers in introducing tea to Europe, shipping it around 1610, and it made its way to Britain by approximately 1650, arriving shortly after coffee had already reached England. The proliferation of tea was facilitated through coffeehouses, where this new beverage gained popularity.

In 1657, an English entrepreneur named Thomas Garway had the innovative idea of serving tea to the public, and it quickly surpassed wines and spirits as the preferred drink of choice.

This transformation posed a challenge for the British government, as they started losing tax revenue from alcohol sales. To address this, a tax on tea was imposed. Nevertheless, it wasn't until the early 18th century that tea became a common beverage among the upper and middle classes.

During the Tang Dynasty(600-900AD), The popularity of tea was rising so high that it eventually got stamped with a tax.

Different variations of tea began to appear and one of those variations was known as black tea. During the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644), a popular way to enjoy tea was to steam it and dry out the loose tea leaves.  However when it came to traveling outside of China, this method of tea did not fare well. So in order to preserve the tea, Chinese merchants would start to roast the tea leaves in order to keep them from them from rotting. The left over leaves that oxidized in the air is what created Black Tea or “Red Tea” is what it’s sometimes called by Chinese natives.
